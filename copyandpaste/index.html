copy and paste and paste and paste
1—

They say the Chinese
are good at copying—

copy and paste
and paste
and paste

Students at the Art Academy of Beijing
detail the same oil painting of
two sunflowers, a pear,
off-brand tributes to Van Gogh—

each brush stroke a brightness
without a name.
2—

The factory knows her face
and from 11pm to 11am
the windows have teeth

She spends her twelve hours
telling the same screw
where to live—
a sinew between speaker
and motherboard

At night, on break
she sends her voice
to her hometown. She tells the moon a wish
about a holiday.

She tells the moon a whisper
about a raise.

3—

In Shanghai,
iPhone factory workers live
eight to a dorm.

A TechInsider reporter says,
in English:

"They play games in the bunks.
They watch TV.
They have unique personalities."
4—

There are 26,800,000 people on Earth
with the surname 吴.
For 王, that number was 92,880,000.

They told me my parents were good
at taking tests.

I feel it in the fervor
with which I replaced mother tongue
with English—

copying lexicon from new culture
installing it into my body.

Beethoven, Bach.
Small yellow fingers play pianissimo,
staccato; the speed of
my mother dreaming in SATs.
5—

By the 3rd grade,
I understood the small, significant
displacement
within my own name

which, translated into Mandarin
becomes prophecy, becomes
凯西, or
To Prosper in the West.

In English, the sounds are
more metal than mother—

Kathys
and Amys
and Jennifers
and Emilys—

amulets to protect us
in the new land.
6—

America opened its border to China in the 1980s. The counter-gesture to what we are experiencing right now.

Many are the only ones in their families to go.
They will tell their children it was meritocracy.

But they were also born in the right place
in the right year.

Tech workers with degrees
making American children,
with American dreams.

7—

My mother draws
lines on black screens
geometries intertwined and laying orthogonal
to the other

The finished product is even smaller
than the white moon of your fingernail,
even smaller
than breath at the end
of a score
8—

The weaving is made of silicon, and it will be able to tell time, or count.
9—

Poorer Chinese migrant workers will weave my mother's design.
They will make it real.

I will never know them,
and they will never know me—
connected by an invisible thread.

Mothers, and daughters,
and daughters,
and daughters.
10—

If you look close enough, the thread is there. You may be holding it in your hand right now.
11—

Bezos will profit. Tim Cook will profit.

Bong Joon Ho will make a great movie about social strata and the horror of contemporary capitalism. And he will profit.

And the workers, we will abstract them all away to machines — not out of cruel individual intention but because corporations prefer this, and they require our locally constrained perceptions of friend, family, community.

Meanwhile, as I write this, we are embroiled in a Cold War with China. Corporations scrambling to control economic giants and cultural capital.
12—

The dissolution of WeChat is yet
another thread.
The machinery keeps going.

The similarities we see
are not coincidental.
They are statistic.

Millions of Chinese children
with bowl cut hair
installing Fur Elise into
their bodies
at the same time.
